Toronto Urban Gardening

an app to foster interest in urban gardening in Toronto's diverse communities

overview

Urban gardening is on the rise in Toronto. Citizens are receptive to the idea of urban gardening but are often not equipped with the resources and connections to get one started.

could an online platform support citizens in learning and sustaining an urban garden within their property?

research

frankly, i wasn’t sure if this was worth the endeavour

About the hypothetical platform

What if there were already accessible resources available (Google, etc.)?
Can an online platform be created to foster the interest and growth of urban garden participants?
What needs to be true for participants to join the online platform in the first place?

Who are the types of people interested in community gardens?
What habits and lifestyles do they have?
What challenges will they face in creating and sustaining a garden?

the answers to these questions began by researching the users and their needs

Wants: Proof that the growth of UA is positive for the community

Needs: Records/tracking/paperwork of each urban garden

Wants: Information and accessibility to an urban garden

Needs: Education, guidance, support



The relationship between the three was such that each needed the other to complete tasks in order to establish and maintain a community garden. Thus the platform focused predominately on the residences, since the time-consuming tasks required changes in policy (updating community garden approval forms) or support beyond the scope of an online platform (direct help from others).



To find where the platform would be most useful and to which user profile I grouped the respective tasks as activities that required: a) immediate results (in yellow) and b) lots of time and interaction (in green)


In order to create basic elements of the platform, research went into users' behaviours and preferences:

- Residences' buying behaviours and how it's influenced by income and education disparities
- Interviews with the community for successful indicators to establish an urban garden
- Attributes of a successful garden and how users ran them

defining metrics

residences had 4 major expectations from the platform

1. Accessible: easy for the whole community to use, including inexperienced users of technology

2. Engaging: ability to show and share active events, educational workshops, recent events, etc.

3. Resourceful: residences need easy access to resources on growing and maintaining an urban garden, and the ability to seek help

4. Visual: photos and videos displayed to present the success and engagement within a garden community

the expectations helped direct the layout of the platform for the best user experience

These four expectations are consistent enough to be metrics. They will only change if the users' initial problems also change. However through usability testing, the layout will have many iterations in order to find the best user experience that meets the four expectations.



navigation and hierarchy

content order was defined by users' level of familiarity

Inactive and active users were grouped based on how familiar they were with gardening. For example, users that had knowledge about gardening but were unfamiliar with the platform were grouped seperately from users that were new to gardening but were active on the platform.

The common tasks (in purple) that each group wanted to complete first were the main features of the platform.

each component must serve a deliberate purpose

Given the wide age-range of users, the process of creating each component was guided by using WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines)'s 4 principles: perceivable, understandable, operable, robustness.

I brainstormed different layouts, envisioning the animation and general feel of the platform and creating a user flowchart.


Navigation was at most two taps to avoid overloading the user:
1 tap to the specific feature
1 tap to view/edit the components of the feature



Defining the home screen

Visually invite users to share their gardening experience

Placed at the bottom because it's the least time-sensitive information.


6. Bottom navigation

Easy access to other important content which requires more real estate.

user interface elements

Keeping colour contrast in mind, I played with colour pallettes and typography that complied with WCAG, and created the remaining layouts.





Minimizing user effort

To keep the onboarding smooth and simple the user is only asked for their location.

Updating sections are on the users' terms and indicated clearly for each section.

Functional animation

Content is deliberately placed within one screen, if possible. For example, events for any day drops down from the calendar on the same screen.


Animations have a functional purpose. Each editable item per section enters from and returns to the right; the direction helps maintain users' sense of orientation.

Buttons provide visual feedback and are intentionally large for clear navigation. Fonts can be increased for the visually impaired.

does an online platform support citizens in sustaining an urban garden?

Yes, but not entirely

Based on research users will likely enjoy exploring the platform since there is little commitment to gardening up front and an encouragement to connect and learn.

However, this is simply the first iteration. Extensive user testing will determine the actual success of the platform. In hindsight to save time, I'd have started testing users with very low fidelity mock ups after setting metrics for measuring expectations.

Furthermore, the online platform is not the ultimate answer to increasing awareness; it is a means to an end and part of a holistic solution to a complex system of people and organizations that rely on each other.
